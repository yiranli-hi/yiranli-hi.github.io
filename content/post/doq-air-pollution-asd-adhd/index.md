Our research on the effects of environmental exposures on neurodevelopmental conditions was featured by [DOQ.nl](https://www.doq.nl/wat-heeft-fijnstof-met-autisme-en-adhd-te-maken/?hash=61ba87bf4177f576150389d84d14bb01), a Dutch platform for healthcare professionals.

## What the study found

Using the [TRAILS](https://www.trails.nl) cohort — a longitudinal study tracking ~2,750 Dutch adolescents from age 10–12 over 16 years — we found that higher exposure to **fine particulate matter (PM2.5)** was associated with more severe autism spectrum disorder (ASD) and ADHD symptoms. Crucially, other air pollutants (NO₂, O₃, SO₂) and environmental noise showed no consistent associations.

The likely mechanism: smaller particles penetrate deeper into the lungs and may reach the brain, triggering neuroinflammatory processes that affect neurodevelopment.

## Why it matters

This study is among the first to track environmental exposures and neurodevelopmental outcomes **longitudinally** across adolescence and early adulthood, rather than focusing only on prenatal or early childhood windows. The findings suggest that reducing PM2.5 exposure could be relevant not just for physical health, but for mental health policy as well.
